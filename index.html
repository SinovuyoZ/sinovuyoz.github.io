The Idea Behind The Work

Most teams diagnose growth problems as marketing problems, sales problems, or product problems.

They're usually none of those — they're gaps that only become visible when you can see across functions at once.

Why This Perspective Is Different

Most specialists work
inside one function.

A career spent across customer acquisition, partnerships, marketing, commercial operations, activation and revenue systems means the constraint is rarely visible from inside any single one of those rooms.

A marketing team sees a conversion problem. Sales sees a lead-quality problem. Product sees an onboarding problem. They're usually describing the same gap.

The value isn't depth in one function. It's standing at the point where functions meet, and seeing what each one misses on its own.

Across different industries and environments, the same pattern kept appearing: capable people, good products, strong intentions — and poor coordination, misaligned incentives, disconnected systems.

The insight was simple. Growth problems are usually coordination problems. Not talent problems. Not effort problems. Coordination problems — hiding in the space between teams that all believe they're doing their job correctly.

The Model

One question.
Six connected lenses.

Not a sequence — a system. Select any lens to see what it shapes, and what shapes it.

How to read this
Growth problems rarely exist in isolation. Each lens influences the others. Explore the system.
Behaviour → shapes Visibility, Activation, Partnerships, Complexity
What customers actually do, as distinct from what a model assumes. Most growth strategies are built on the second and break on the first — and that gap quietly distorts everything downstream.
Visibility → shapes Behaviour, Activation, Complexity
A strong product with weak commercial architecture is invisible. Visibility isn't awareness — it's whether the right customer finds the right offer in the right context. Get this wrong and activation never gets a fair test.
Activation → shapes Behaviour, Visibility, Revenue, Complexity
Revenue is an output. The work is finding which stage — acquisition, activation, retention — is quietly broken, and fixing that one stage rather than rebuilding everything around it.
Revenue → shapes Activation, Partnerships, Complexity
Revenue performance determines which partnerships get attention and resources — and which activation problems get treated as urgent versus theoretical.
Partnerships → shapes Behaviour, Revenue, Complexity
Relationships compound or they don't, depending on whether the structure rewards mutual outcomes — not just transaction volume. A misaligned partnership quietly reshapes customer behaviour too.
Complexity → shapes all five lenses
In regulated or multi-market environments, execution fails without alignment first. Knowing who needs to move, and in what order, is itself the skill — and it touches every lens above.

Where This Shows Up

Six places growth
quietly breaks.

Each looks like an isolated issue from inside its own function. Together, they're usually the same gap in different forms.

Customer Activation
Looks Like
A sign-up that never becomes a funded, active customer. Often blamed on "low intent."
Usually Is
Friction at a specific step nobody owns — between marketing's promise and product's onboarding.
Revenue Leakage
Looks Like
Acquisition numbers are healthy, but revenue isn't growing at the same rate.
Usually Is
One stage of the journey — often invisible in top-line reporting — quietly losing most of the value.
Strategic Partnerships
Looks Like
A partnership that exists on paper but never produces the expected volume.
Usually Is
No shared accountability for outcomes — both sides assumed the other would drive it.
GTM Execution
Looks Like
A strategy that sounded right in the room but doesn't show up in the market.
Usually Is
A translation gap between what leadership decided and what the front line actually does differently.
Commercial Visibility
Looks Like
Strong product, flat growth, and a team convinced the answer is "more marketing."
Usually Is
The right customer can't find or recognise the offer in the context they're in.
Cross-Functional Alignment
Looks Like
Every team can show a dashboard that says things are fine.
Usually Is
Each dashboard is measuring something slightly different, and none of them is the customer's experience.
Patterns, Not Symptoms

What this usually
looks like from inside.

01
Ambiguity in success
Everyone is busy. No one can confidently say whether the business is actually improving, because the metrics don't connect to a shared story about outcomes.
02
Pipeline disconnection
Top-of-funnel looks healthy. Revenue doesn't follow. Something between the first touch and the close is leaking, and it has never been mapped end to end.
03
Fragmented truths
Sales, marketing and product each have a credible version of what's happening — and the three versions don't agree, because they were never built on the same definitions.
04
Strategic paralysis
Several plausible growth paths are on the table, and the organisation can't commit to one — usually because the actual constraint hasn't been named yet.

Where The Thinking Holds

The constraint moves.
The question doesn't.

New tools and platforms change where coordination breaks down — not whether it does.

Trust
Every new channel or platform resets the question of why a customer should believe a claim — and who, internally, owns that answer.
Identity
As products get more personalised, "knowing the customer" becomes a coordination problem between teams holding different pieces of who they are.
Incentives
Misaligned incentives are the quiet cause behind most of the gaps in this framework — and the hardest one to redesign once it's embedded.
Automation
Automating a broken handoff just makes the gap faster and harder to see. The underlying misalignment has to be fixed first.
Communities
When value is created between users rather than delivered by a company, the seams move — but they don't disappear.

Tools change every few years. The shape of the gap doesn't.
